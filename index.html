Population Overview since 1980

Since 1901, Australia's population has substantially increased both nationally and at the state and territory level.Australia’s 2021 population is 6.8 times larger than in 1901, increasing from 3.8 to 25.7 million.The national population has grown every year, excluding WWI, since 1901.The highest net population growth occurred in 2023.

Focusing on Population Trends from 2013 to 2023

Net overseas migration has been a significant driver of Australia's population growth, accounting for over half of the total increase throughout the period observed. The numbers rose dramatically from a net inflow of 30,000 individuals in 1992–93 to 241,000 in 2018–19. However, the COVID-19 pandemic brought unprecedented changes with international border closures leading to the first net outflow since World War II, recording a decrease of 85,000 in 2020–21. Concurrently, natural increase (births minus deaths) contributed nearly half of the demographic growth, although it has seen a decline—from 139,000 individuals in 1992–93 to 106,000 in 2022–23. This shift reflects the decreasing fertility rates, down from 1.86 to 1.58 babies per woman over the same timeframe, and an ageing population, as the number of deaths has begun to surpass the number of births.

Detailed State Analysis

Australia’s capital cities tend to be younger and age more slowly than regional areas. This is mainly because capital cities have historically attracted a larger share of overseas migrants, who tend to be younger than the overall population.

Since its federation, Australia has experienced significant aging across its population, marked by a gradual increase in the median age from 22.5 years in 1901 to 38.4 years in 2023. Notably, females have consistently outnumbered males since 1978, reflecting broader demographic trends.
Over the years, the 5-9 years age group has seen the largest decrease as a proportion of the total population, while the 60-64 age group has witnessed the most substantial increase.

Net overseas migration has been a significant factor in Australia's population growth, particularly before the COVID-19 pandemic. However, the pandemic led to the first net outflow of -85,000 in 2020-21 due to border restrictions. As restrictions eased, migration rebounded to 204,000 in 2021-22 and surged to 528,000 by 2022-23.
Natural increase has also played a role, although its impact has diminished relative to total population growth. Fertility rates have dropped from 1.86 in 1992-93 to 1.58 in 2022-23, with life expectancy increasing.
Internal migration has historically been high in Australia but saw significant reductions during the pandemic. Movement between states fell sharply in 2020, only partially recovering by 2022.
