Australia Population Visualization

Population Overview since 1980

Australia's population growth has been influenced by significant global and national events over the past century.
Beginning with World War I in 1914, a noticeable decline in population growth is observed, likely due to the impacts of war-related mobilization and fatalities.
After World War II ended in 1945, there was a rebound in population growth, which can be attributed to the post-war recovery, increased birth rates during the baby boom, and boosted immigration.
The Global Financial Crisis in 2008 again shows a slight decrease in population growth, reflecting the economic strain that could have curtailed migration and lowered birth rates.
Most recently, the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 led to a sharp and significant drop in population growth, primarily due to stringent border closures and the broader impacts of the health crisis on society.

Focusing on Population Trends from 2013 to 2023

The visual representation of the average population across Australian states and territories highlights the demographic distribution and variations within the region.
New South Wales (NSW) emerges as the most populous state, shown in the darkest shade of green, reflecting its high population density.
This is followed by Victoria and Queensland, which also display significant population densities, illustrated by their relatively darker shades.
In contrast, the Northern Territory and Tasmania are depicted with much lighter shades, indicating their lower population figures.

Detailed State Analysis

Australia’s capital cities tend to be younger and age more slowly than regional areas. This is mainly because capital cities have historically attracted a larger share of overseas migrants, who tend to be younger than the overall population.

Since its federation, Australia has experienced significant aging across its population, marked by a gradual increase in the median age from 22.5 years in 1901 to 38.4 years in 2023. Notably, females have consistently outnumbered males since 1978, reflecting broader demographic trends.

Net overseas migration was the major contributor to change in most states, New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia, Western Australia, Tasmania and the Australian Capital Territory. natural increase was the major contributor to change in the Northern Territory.
