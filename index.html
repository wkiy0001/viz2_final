Australia Population Visualization

Population Overview since 1980

Australia's population growth has been influenced by significant global and national events over the past century.

  1. World War I (1914): Decline due to war mobilization and fatalities.
  2. Great Depression: Reduced immigration and increased unemployment slowed growth.
  3. World War II (Ended in 1945): Rebound due to post-war recovery and increased immigration.
  4. Global Financial Crisis (2008): Slight decrease in growth from economic strain.
  5. COVID-19 Pandemic (2020): Sharp drop due to border closures and health crisis impacts.

Focusing on Population Trends from 2013 to 2023

New South Wales (NSW) emerges as the most populous state, shown in the darkest shade of green, reflecting its high population density.

This is followed by Victoria and Queensland, which also display significant population densities, illustrated by their relatively darker shades.

In contrast, the Northern Territory and Tasmania are depicted with much lighter shades, indicating their lower population figures.

The line chart enables you to select and inspect demographic trends over key periods:

  • Pre-Pandemic (2013-2019): Population growth was stable, with net overseas migration regularly surpassing natural increase.
  • During the Pandemic (2020-2021): A sharp decrease in net overseas migration, a result of strict border closures.
  • Post-Pandemic (2022-2023): Following the reopening of borders, there was a swift upsurge in net overseas migration.

Detailed State Analysis

The interactive map helps you to explore population of local government areas (LGAs) in a selected year and state. The map highlights the 10 cities with the highest populations, prominently annotating them to showcase key urban centers driving demographic changes.

Since its federation, Australia has experienced significant aging across its population, marked by a gradual increase in the median age from 35 years in 2013 to 38.4 years in 2023. Nationally, females have outnumbered males.
